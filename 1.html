Background

Anaphylaxis is a life threatening allergic reaction that can occur seconds or minutes after a person is exposed to an allergen [1]. When someone goes into anaphylaxis, chemicals rush throughout, sending the person into shock. Epinephrine, the medicine in the Epipen, needs to be administered immediately to relieve the symptoms.

Signs of Anaphylaxis:

  • Trouble breathing
  • Low blood pressure
  • Increased heart rate
  • Hives
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting

Where to find an Epipen

Many people with life threatening allergies will carry an Epipen or other epinephrine autoadministrator with them. In addition, many public facilities and places with programming for children will have a dose of epinephrine on their premises. To find them, ask a worker or a security guard.

Administering the dose

  1. Take the Epipen out of the container.

  2. Hold the Epipen vertically with the blue safety cap upwards. Remove the blue safety cap with the other hand. To remember the way to hold the Epipen, think: Blue cap to the blue sky.


  3. Hold the Epipen in a fist with the orange tip downwards.

  4. Lift the Epipen up, as if creating a snow angel, and then quickly bring the Epipen into the upper thigh.

  5. Hold the Epipen in place for three seconds.

  6. Remove the Epipen from the leg.

  7. If symptoms do not improve within five to fifteen minutes, administer a secondary dose.

After administering

After a dose of an EpiPen, the victim must immediately be transported to the emergency room. Epinephrine treats the most threatening symptoms, such as throat swelling and breathing troubles, but anaphylaxis can rebound hours or days after the Epipen is administered. As such, a person experiencing a severe allergy event must be monitored for four to eight hours in an emergency room, and then sent home with an additional dose of epinephrine.

Common Errors

Check the expiration date of the epinephrine before administering. In addition, be sure that the orange tip is downwards, as incorrectly holding the Epipen can lead to the needle ejecting in an incorrect direction.
